Transgender - Non Religious

Giving bathroom rights to transgender people puts cisgender women in danger

1. Bans on transgender people from using the correct bathroom are misguided, for starters, these bans don't protect cisgender women. If someone wanted to sexually assault women in a bathroom, be it a transgender woman or a cisgender man dressed as a woman, a bathroom ban would not deter them. Sexual assault is already illegal with harsh penalties. So if someone tries to sexually assault people regardless of the punishments, the much smaller penalties imposed by a bathroom ban would do nothing to stop them. Research indicates that policies that ban transgender bathroom discrimination do not affect the number of sexual assaults.
2. While bathroom bans do nothing to help cisgender women, they do serious harm to transgender people. Implementing bathroom bans would put transgender people in an unwinnable situation. They could use the bathroom matching their assigned sex at birth, which would open them up to sexual assault and harassment. A study found schools with discriminatory bathroom policies had to increase in sexual assaults for transgender youth. They could use the bathroom matching their gender identity, which would put them at risk of legal trouble. Finally, they could detransition in public which would lead to increased gender dysphoria, worse mental health and damage their freedom of expression.
3. Research links bathroom bans to worse mental health for transgender people. One study found that transgender youth who faced bathroom discrimination were more likely to consider suicide and have worse mental health. Research also suggests that transgender-inclusive environments which would include bathroom accommodations lead to better mental health for transgender people.
4. Many experts and groups oppose bathroom bans. An open letter signed by many sexual assault prevention organizations opposed bathroom bans. Many medical groups like the American Medical Association and the American Academy of Pediatrics also oppose bathroom restrictions.
5. If bathroom bans were implemented, they would be very hard to enforce. Using IDs would be impractical and going off looks would fail to catch many people. Enforcing bathroom bans would also waste useful police resources that would have been better spent fighting real sexual predators.
6. Another issue with enforcing bathroom bans and with transgender bathroom discrimination in general is that it would affect cisgender people. Because looks would be the only practical way to enforce bathroom bans, cisgender women who do not look feminine enough or cisgender men who do not look masculine enough would be kicked from bathrooms and discriminated against. This has happened before, there are real cases of cisgender women be harassed for using the women's bathroom after being mistaken for being transgender women. This type of side effect of bathroom bans would also hurt freedom of expression.
7. Transgender people have had access to bathrooms for years with little issue. Even though isolated cases of transgender people assaulting people in bathrooms exist, this is not an excuse to pass bathroom bans because the actions of one person don't represent an entire group.

Transgender - Non Religious

Rapid onset gender dysphoria

1. Rapid-onset gender dysphoria (ROGD) is a disputed diagnosis and theory that gender dysphoria and being transgender is a social contagion that spreads through peer influence. It's often used by people to invalidate transgender identity or explain the rise in transgender identification. Much of the fuss around ROGD arose because of a study popularized that term and claimed to have observed it. The study surveyed parents of transgender kids and concluded based on responses that the kids had ROGD. However, this study has some major flaws.
2. First of all, the study surveyed parents, not their kids. Because transgender kids are often quiet about their identities, parents are not the best source of information. This is especially true for the rapid onset part of ROGD the study claimed to observe. Because people are often silent about their identities until coming out, it might only seem like rapid onset to someone watching.
3. All of this is made worse by the fact the source of data was biased. The parents in the study were recruited from anti-transgender websites like transgender trend and 4thwavenow. This would make an unreliable source of data about transgender kids even more unreliable. While some may claim that most of the parents were actually supportive of their child because 88.2% said they supported transgender rights, this assertion has some issues. First, many might have interpreted the question to mean general protections like nondiscrimination laws and not more nuanced things like whether they accepted their child. Second and more damming, 76.5% of parents thought their child was incorrect in identifying as transgender.
4. The study also has other issues. For example, it made no mention of best-practice methods for conducting surveys and gave parents a simplified and un-verified version of the DSM-5 to "diagnose" their kids with gender dysphoria.
5. The study's conclusions and many claims about ROGD in general also seem flawed. It was suggested that adolescents consuming transgender-related social media make them transgender, but it could easily be the opposite. If someone found out they were transgender or were questioning their gender identity, they would probably seek advice or information online. This reasoning also addresses the claim that transgender friends can make someone transgender. Because if someone is transgender they might seek out friends who have that thing in common, or if someone has a transgender friend, they might be more comfortable coming out.
6. Some research has found issues with the notion of ROGD. One study was not able to replicate some of ROGD's predictions concluding, "We did not find support within a clinical population for a new etiologic phenomenon of rapid onset gender dysphoria during adolescence."
7. Another issue with ROGD is that it frames people finding out they are transgender during or after puberty with little signs before puberty as novel and evidence of social contagion. However, this reasoning is flawed because this phenomenon is not novel and has been observed before all the fuss about social contagion. It is also not good evidence in favor of social contagion because puberty often causes people to discover their gender identity or worsens dysphoria.
8. Many transgender people also find out about their identity before puberty which is not predicted by ROGD. One study found that most transgender adults first began experiencing gender dysphoria between ages 3 and 7.
10. It's hard to think that social factors can push someone to identify as transgender given all the hardship transgender people have to go through and the magnitude of the decision.
11. Many medical organizations have opposed the use of ROGD citing the lack of medical evidence.
12. All of this is not to say ROGD does not exist in any way whatsoever, there is just no good evidence for ROGD individuals making up a large portion of the transgender population like some claim. Even if ROGD was real and significant, it would not change too much. Arguments for transgender rights and protections are not hurt by the way someone becomes transgender. Things that might need to be changed would be the caution taken by people transitioning and the way people are educated about transgender issues.
